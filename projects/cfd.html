A thermal modeling package designed for solving complex heat conduction problems

This Python package provides comprehensive thermal modeling capabilities for steady-state and transient thermal analysis, with specialized capabilities for modeling ablative materials used in spacecraft heat shields. The solvers handle 1-3D geometries, Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions, and material phase changes with high accuracy and computational efficiency.

Key Features

Steady State Analysis

1D and 2D steady-state thermal solvers with support for user input boundary conditions (Dirichlet and Neumann), material properties, and source terms (heat generation/heat sink)

Transient Analysis

2D transient thermal solvers with explicit time-stepping (first-order temporal, second-order spatial accuracy)

3D Cylindrical Heat Shield Modeling

Specialized cylindrical coordinate solver for reentry heat shield analysis

Advanced Ablation Physics

Sophisticated material sublimation model tracking latent heat accumulation, surface recession, and transient boundary evolution

Demonstrated Use Case: 3D Ablative Heat Shield Model

The Mission

Model the performance of a circular ablative heat shield to ensure it can protect a spacecraft from extreme heat during reentry.

Model Methodology

Heat Transfer Equation

Assuming internal gas convection is negligible, the differential equation we seek to solve is:

$$ \underset{\text{Storage}}{\rho c_p \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}} = \underset{\text{Diffusion}}{\nabla \cdot (k \nabla T)} + \underset{\text{Pyrolysis}}{\Delta H_r \,\frac{d\rho}{dt}} $$
$$ \frac{d\rho}{dt} = - k_0 \exp\!\left(-\frac{E}{RT}\right) (\rho - \rho_r) $$

This equation includes the terms for storage, diffusion, and pyrolysis. The pyrolysis term is responsible for the ablation of the material, and is derived from the Arrhenius equation. The Arrhenius equation is a mathematical model that describes the rate of a chemical reaction as a function of temperature.

In order to model the destruction of the material, it is assumed all materials are destroyed via sublimation. A threshold sublimation temperature is used. When the temperature of a node reaches the threshold temperature, the temperature does not raise. Instead all additional thermal energy goes to latent heat of sublimation. When the latent heat threshold is reached, the node is marked as destroyed. The flux condition is then applied to the node directly below and the surface is updated.

Sublimation temperature: Tsub [K]

Latent heat of sublimation: ŒîHsub [J/kg]

In order to model the heat flux applied to the surface, we use a flux (Neumann) boundary condition of the form:

$$ - k \frac{\partial T}{\partial z} = q''_{\text{applied}} $$

Numerical Methods

  • Transient Analysis: Forward difference time and centered spacial differencing used to explicitly solve the heat equation at each time step (first-order temporal, second-order spatial accuracy)
  • Ablative Material Physics:
    • Materials sublimate when sublimation temperature is reached
    • Temperature clamped at sublimation point; excess heat accumulates as latent heat
    • Material destroyed when latent heat threshold is met
  • Transient Boundary Evolution:
    • Neumann boundary conditions applied via ghost nodes at surface
    • Boundary condition dynamically moves to next node as surface material is destroyed
    • Boolean arrays track surface, interior, and destroyed node states

Results

Methodology

The solver employs finite difference methods (FDM) to discretize the heat conduction equation. For steady-state problems, the Poisson equation is solved using iterative methods, while transient problems use implicit time-stepping for numerical stability.

The ablation model implements a sophisticated energy balance approach: when material elements reach the sublimation temperature, latent heat is accumulated until sufficient energy is available for phase change. The surface then recedes, and boundary conditions are dynamically updated to reflect the new geometry.

Grid convergence is ensured through proper scaling of source terms and validation studies. The cylindrical coordinate system enables accurate modeling of axisymmetric geometries common in aerospace applications.

Usage Example

Here's how to run a 3D heat shield simulation:

from cfd.solvers.ablative_heat_shield import solve_heat_shield_3d

# Run 3D cylindrical heat shield simulation
r, theta, z, transient = solve_heat_shield_3d(
    Nr=50, Ntheta=50, Nz=50,      # Grid resolution
    R=80, thickness=0.05,          # Geometry parameters
    time=150, dt=1,                # Time stepping
    k=0.04, rho=270, cp=1100,      # Material properties
    initial_temp=0,                # Initial conditions
    sub_temp=3500,                 # Sublimation temperature
    sub_heat=2500000               # Latent heat of sublimation
)
