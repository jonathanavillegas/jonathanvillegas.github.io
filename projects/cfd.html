A thermal modeling package designed for solving complex heat conduction problems

This Python package provides comprehensive thermal modeling capabilities for steady-state and transient thermal analysis, with specialized capabilities for modeling ablative materials used in spacecraft heat shields. The solvers handle 1-3D geometries, Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions, and material phase changes.

Key Features

Steady State Analysis

1D and 2D steady-state thermal solvers with support for user input boundary conditions (Dirichlet and Neumann), material properties, and source terms (heat generation/heat sink)

Transient Analysis

2D transient thermal solvers with explicit time-stepping (first-order temporal, second-order spatial accuracy)

3D Cylindrical Heat Shield Modeling

Specialized cylindrical coordinate solver for reentry heat shield analysis

Advanced Ablation Physics

Material pyrolysis and sublimation model tracking latent heat accumulation, surface recession, and transient boundary evolution

Demonstrated Use Case: 3D Ablative Heat Shield Model

The Mission

Model the performance of a circular ablative heat shield to ensure it can protect a spacecraft from extreme heat during reentry.

Model Methodology

Heat Transfer Equation

Assuming internal gas convection is negligible, the following differential equation is used to model the thermal behavior of the material:

$$ \underset{\text{Storage}}{\rho c_p \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}} = \underset{\text{Diffusion}}{\nabla \cdot (k \nabla T)} + \underset{\text{Pyrolysis}}{\Delta H_r \,\frac{d\rho}{dt}} $$
$$ \frac{d\rho}{dt} = - k_0 \exp\!\left(-\frac{E}{RT}\right) (\rho - \rho_r) $$

This equation includes the terms for storage, diffusion, and pyrolysis. The pyrolysis term is responsible for the ablation of the material, and is derived from the Arrhenius equation. The Arrhenius equation is a mathematical model that describes the rate of a chemical reaction as a function of temperature.

To model the destruction of the material, it is assumed all materials are destroyed via sublimation. A threshold sublimation temperature is used. When the temperature of a node reaches the threshold temperature, the temperature does not raise. Instead all additional thermal energy goes to latent heat of sublimation. When the latent heat threshold is reached, the node is marked as destroyed. The flux condition is then applied to the node directly below and the surface is updated.

Sublimation temperature: Tsub [K]

Latent heat of sublimation: ŒîHsub [J/kg]

To model the heat flux applied to the surface, a flux (Neumann) boundary condition is used of the form:

$$ - k \frac{\partial T}{\partial z} = q''_{\text{applied}} $$

Discretization

Cylindrical coordinates (r, Œ∏, z) model the circular geometry in 3D. Central difference scheme provides second-order spatial accuracy in all directions. Special handling at r=0 using L'H√¥pital's rule avoids coordinate singularity.

Numerical Methods

  • Transient Analysis: Forward difference time and centered spacial differencing used to explicitly solve the heat equation at each time step (first-order temporal, second-order spatial accuracy)
  • Ablative Material Physics:
    • Materials sublimate when sublimation temperature is reached
    • Temperature clamped at sublimation point; excess heat accumulates as latent heat
    • Material destroyed when latent heat threshold is met
  • Transient Boundary Evolution:
    • Neumann boundary conditions applied via ghost nodes at surface
    • Boundary condition dynamically moves to next node as surface material is destroyed
    • Boolean arrays track surface, interior, and destroyed node states

Results

Model Efficacy

  • 2nd Order Spatial Accuracy: 1D and 2D steady-state solvers are second-order accurate in space. Ghost nodes are employed to handle flux boundary conditions, maintaining second-order accuracy at the boundary.
  • 1st Order Temporal Accuracy: 2D transient solvers are first-order accurate in time. Models are designed for small time steps to ensure accuracy.
  • Hand Calculations: 1D steady-state thermal solvers were validated using hand calculation with a variety of boundary conditions. Results were compared to the simulation results and the error was found to be less than 1%.
  • Grid Convergence: Solvers were run with different grid resolutions and the results were compared. Cases were found to be within 1% difference.

Future Work

  • 2D Transient and Steady-State Validation: 2D transient and steady-state solvers will be validated against commerical solvers (Thermal Desktop).
  • Ablation Modeling Validation: Ablation modeling will be validated against NASA's Porous-material Analysis Toolbox (PATO)
  • Non-Symmetric Heat Flux Distribution: Heat shields entering at non-zero attack angles will be modeled, taking advantage of the solver's 3D capabilities.
  • Higher Order Accuracy: Higher order finite difference schemes will be implemented to improve the accuracy of the solvers.
  • Iterative Solving Methods: Higher order schemes often necessitate the use of iterative/implicit solving methods (ie. Gauss-Seidel method)to handle the computational cost.
